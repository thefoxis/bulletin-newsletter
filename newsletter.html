From the desk of Karolina Szczur

Your work doesn’t equal your worth

Over the last few months, I’ve been thinking a lot about work and its place in our lives. Many feel most themselves while fulfilled by their work. Their identities are tightly tied to their job persona—an engineer, designer, writer, founder or what have you. The level of contentment with life itself becomes a vector of professional success. For a while, I’ve fallen into this trap, too.

An important title, a significant following on social media, higher salary, more articles we publish, talks we give, it all contributes to a sense of accomplishment and validation of our worth. We feel important. Our work matters. An urgent need to maintain personal brands emerges. Always be at peak performance. Rest is for the weak-minded. Hustle on.

When you’re constantly expected to be achieving, it’s harder to make a distinction between work and play. Everything merges under a nebulous notion of „creativity” and constant output. As Oli Mould says—„our entire productive selves and the relationships we keep are now geared towards producing things, ideas, experiences and services that capitalism can exploit.”

We can’t escape capitalism but we can control how we approach life. There’s a beautiful Japanese concept—ikigai (生き甲斐), which roughly translates to „reason for being”. An intersection of values, purpose, fulfilment and true self which leads to a happy life. For many of us, our jobs are our ikigai, but instead of joy, we have found burn out, stress, anxiety and, in result, broken relationships. Success is a falsifiable god.

Whatever your job is, it doesn’t define your worth. It also doesn’t have to be the source of realisation. The tech industry wrongly glorifies those who fully commit to workism. I’ll be finding a different ikigai.
